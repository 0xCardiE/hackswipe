# Website Trust & Conversion Reference Framework

Use this reference when the main skill needs additional detail.

## 1. The successful-visit principle

A successful visit is not necessarily a long visit.

A visitor may succeed by:

- finding a phone number and calling;
- confirming the service area;
- reading a direct answer;
- downloading a file;
- checking compatibility;
- deciding the product is not suitable;
- completing a purchase or booking.

Avoid designing artificial paths merely to increase page views, clicks, scrolling, or session duration.

## 2. Trust and conversion stages

A commercial page generally moves through:

1. **Recognition** — the page matches the visitor's intent.
2. **Comprehension** — the offer and expected outcome are understood.
3. **Credibility** — evidence makes the offer believable.
4. **Evaluation** — the visitor can assess fit, cost, risk, and alternatives.
5. **Action** — the appropriate next step is obvious and low-friction.

## 3. Familiarity

Use conventional patterns for:

- logo placement;
- navigation;
- menu labels;
- CTA appearance;
- pricing presentation;
- forms;
- contact information;
- footer links.

Innovation belongs in the value proposition and product experience. Basic navigation should rarely require learning.

## 4. Progressive disclosure

Organize information into three levels.

### Scan

- headline;
- section titles;
- key benefit;
- price;
- proof;
- CTA.

### Understand

- short explanations;
- screenshots;
- steps;
- comparisons;
- examples.

### Investigate

- detailed documentation;
- technical methodology;
- policies;
- long-form case studies;
- legal and security details.

Do not hide decision-critical facts such as major limitations, recurring billing, compatibility, or material conditions.

## 5. Proof hierarchy

Prefer evidence in roughly this order:

1. Demonstrable product or result
2. Specific customer outcome
3. Verifiable third-party feedback
4. Named case study
5. Real usage data
6. Relevant credential
7. Transparent method
8. Genuine company identity
9. Detailed privacy or security explanation
10. Honest limitations

A few strong proof points are better than a wall of weak badges.

## 6. Homepage section library

Choose only relevant sections.

### Header

Purpose: orientation and access.

Keep primary navigation to approximately three to six items where possible.

### Hero

Purpose: establish fit, outcome, action, and immediate reassurance.

### Proof strip

Purpose: answer “Why should I believe this?” quickly.

### Problem recognition

Purpose: demonstrate understanding of the visitor's current situation.

### How it works

Purpose: make the process feel understandable and achievable.

Three steps are often sufficient.

### Demonstration

Purpose: show the product, service, or completed result.

### Benefits

Purpose: connect capabilities to practical and larger outcomes.

Pattern:

**Feature → practical result → larger benefit**

### Useful value

Purpose: provide help before requesting commitment.

Examples:

- calculator;
- checklist;
- sample output;
- compatibility checker;
- transparent comparison;
- concise guide.

### Social proof

Purpose: reduce uncertainty with relevant experiences from others.

### Comparison

Purpose: help visitors evaluate alternatives fairly.

Admit when another option is appropriate.

### Fit and non-fit

Purpose: qualify visitors and increase credibility.

### Pricing

Purpose: remove cost uncertainty.

Explain price, billing, inclusions, limitations, tax treatment where relevant, refund terms, and what happens next.

### FAQ

Purpose: resolve actual objections.

Do not use it as a keyword-storage section.

### Final CTA

Purpose: invite action after the visitor has acquired context and trust.

### Footer

Purpose: verification, support, contact, legal, and secondary navigation.

## 7. CTA rules

Use one dominant primary CTA per page.

Good CTA labels describe the action or result:

- Start free
- Download the exporter
- Check availability
- Get an estimate
- Book a consultation
- Install the extension
- View the sample report
- Call now

Avoid:

- Submit
- Continue
- Explore
- Discover
- Click here
- Learn more

A secondary CTA should support users who need more information without competing visually.

## 8. Common trust killers

### Visual

- broken or stretched images;
- inconsistent fonts;
- poor alignment;
- low-resolution logos;
- layout shifts;
- mobile overflow;
- excessive popups;
- fake chat notifications;
- multiple unrelated design styles;
- irrelevant stock imagery.

### Content

- unsupported superlatives;
- fake urgency;
- hidden pricing;
- missing ownership or contact information;
- copied generic content;
- inconsistent company names;
- vague security promises;
- fabricated testimonials;
- missing limitations;
- obsolete information.

### Conversion

- long forms before value;
- account creation too early;
- unclear next step;
- too many equal CTAs;
- surprise checkout costs;
- autoplay media;
- inaccessible phone or contact action;
- forced demo before pricing.

## 9. Copy patterns

### Outcome headline

`[Achieve result] without [major frustration or risk]`

Use only when both parts are concrete.

### Context label

A small label can clarify category:

- Genome report export tool
- Accounting for Croatian small businesses
- Emergency plumbing in Rijeka

### Reassurance line

Examples:

- No credit card required
- One-time payment
- Cancel anytime
- Files stay on your device
- Free estimate
- Response within one business day

Only use truthful claims.

### Problem-to-solution card

**Problem:** a recognizable frustration or risk.

**Change:** the specific improvement created by the product or service.

### Specific testimonial

Include:

- starting situation;
- relevant use;
- observable result;
- identity or context where consent allows.

## 10. Visual hierarchy

Clearly distinguish:

- H1;
- H2;
- H3;
- body copy;
- supporting text;
- primary CTA;
- secondary action;
- links;
- labels;
- captions.

When everything is emphasized, nothing is emphasized.

Group related elements closely and separate different concepts visibly.

## 11. Interaction quality

Good interactions assist a decision or task:

- estimate cost;
- compare plans;
- preview an output;
- select a use case;
- check eligibility;
- choose a time;
- filter services;
- upload an input;
- copy a useful result.

Bad interactions exist mainly to create motion or clicks.

## 12. Measurement framework

### Acquisition

- source;
- query or campaign;
- landing page;
- intent match.

### Comprehension

- relevant demo use;
- pricing views;
- meaningful section interaction;
- navigation to expected information.

### Trust

- case study use;
- review inspection;
- privacy or security information;
- contact and company verification.

### Conversion

- primary CTA;
- call;
- form start and completion;
- checkout;
- purchase;
- install;
- download;
- booking;
- activation.

### Quality

- refund rate;
- lead quality;
- activation;
- task completion;
- support demand;
- repeat use;
- customer satisfaction.

## 13. Testing scripts

### Five-second test

Ask:

- What does this company offer?
- Who is it for?
- What result does it provide?
- What would you do next?
- What seemed untrustworthy?

### Findability test

Ask the participant to find:

- price;
- process;
- compatibility;
- refund terms;
- contact information;
- whether the service fits their case.

### Completion test

Ask the participant to complete the primary conversion and observe hesitation, confusion, errors, and missing reassurance.

### Objection interview

Ask:

- What would stop you?
- What is missing?
- Which claim is difficult to believe?
- What would you verify?
- What feels risky?
- What seems unnecessarily complicated?

## 14. Specialized variations

### SaaS

Prioritize:

- product screenshot;
- time-to-value;
- integration or compatibility;
- trial and billing details;
- security;
- activation path.

### Local service

Prioritize:

- location and service area;
- phone CTA;
- availability;
- real team and work;
- licenses or insurance where relevant;
- process;
- price expectations;
- reviews.

### Ecommerce

Prioritize:

- product truthfulness;
- photography;
- dimensions and compatibility;
- delivery cost and timing;
- returns;
- reviews;
- checkout confidence.

### High-value B2B

Prioritize:

- business outcome;
- case studies;
- process;
- team expertise;
- implementation risk;
- scope;
- consultation or demo;
- qualification.

### Sensitive health, financial, legal, or genomic service

Prioritize:

- responsible claims;
- limitations;
- professional boundaries;
- data handling;
- security;
- consent;
- deletion;
- legal terms;
- appropriate expert review.

## 15. Final review questions

Before considering a page complete, ask:

1. Can a new visitor explain the offer after five seconds?
2. Does the first viewport match the likely intent?
3. Is the primary CTA obvious?
4. Is evidence present before major commitment?
5. Are cost and risk sufficiently clear?
6. Is the process understandable?
7. Are important limitations visible?
8. Does the mobile experience preserve hierarchy?
9. Can keyboard and assistive-technology users complete the task?
10. Is the page fast and stable?
11. Does every section help recognition, understanding, trust, comparison, decision, or action?
12. Are any claims unsupported?
13. Is there anything present only to manufacture engagement?
