# Website Trust & Conversion Architect

Use this skill whenever the user asks to create, redesign, improve, audit, critique, or plan a commercial website or landing page.

The objective is not to maximize artificial engagement. The objective is to help the right visitor:

1. recognize that they are in the right place;
2. understand the offer;
3. trust the business;
4. evaluate whether the offer fits;
5. complete the appropriate next action with minimal friction.

Read `references/framework.md` when you need the full framework, detailed checks, page structures, or examples.

## Core operating principles

Always:

- Match the visitor's likely intent immediately.
- Use familiar web conventions unless deviation clearly improves usability.
- Reduce uncertainty before asking for commitment.
- Present essential information before details.
- Use progressive disclosure for technical or secondary information.
- Prefer real evidence over decorative persuasion.
- Use one dominant primary action per page.
- Make limitations, pricing, compatibility, privacy, and next steps clear.
- Design mobile deliberately rather than shrinking desktop layouts.
- Treat accessibility, performance, and clarity as trust features.
- Write for scanning first and deeper reading second.
- Give users useful information before asking them to buy or submit data.
- Make every interaction help the user understand, compare, decide, or act.

Never:

- manufacture clicks, scrolling, or time on page;
- hide essential information to increase engagement;
- use fake scarcity, fake urgency, fake reviews, or unverifiable claims;
- overload pages with equal-priority calls to action;
- use jargon where plain language works;
- prioritize visual novelty over comprehension;
- add animation without a functional purpose;
- make unsupported claims such as “100% secure” or “guaranteed results”;
- assume long time on page or deep scrolling automatically means success.

## Required discovery

Before designing, infer or identify:

- website type;
- primary audience;
- visitor's main intent;
- desired conversion;
- commitment and risk level;
- likely objections;
- available evidence and trust assets;
- important constraints such as legal, privacy, health, financial, or security concerns.

Do not block progress when information is missing. State reasonable assumptions and produce a useful first version.

## Page strategy

For each important page, define:

- **Primary visitor intent**
- **Primary conversion**
- **Secondary action**
- **Main objection**
- **Required proof**
- **Information that must appear before the CTA**
- **Information that can be progressively disclosed**

A page should answer:

1. Am I in the right place?
2. What is being offered?
3. Who is it for?
4. What result can I expect?
5. Why should I believe this?
6. How does it work?
7. What will it cost or require?
8. What are the limitations or risks?
9. What should I do next?
10. What happens after I act?

## Default homepage sequence

Use this as a starting point, not a rigid template:

1. Familiar header
2. Hero with outcome, audience, CTA, and reassurance
3. Immediate proof strip
4. Problem recognition
5. Three-step explanation
6. Product, service, or result demonstration
7. Major benefits connected to features
8. Useful interactive or educational element
9. Social proof or case study
10. Fair comparison or alternative analysis
11. Who it is and is not for
12. Clear pricing or cost expectations
13. Objection-handling FAQ
14. Final CTA with reassurance
15. Verification-focused footer

Remove sections that do not help the visitor make a decision. Add specialized sections when the business model requires them.

## Hero requirements

The first viewport should make clear:

- what this is;
- who it is for;
- the concrete result;
- the primary next action;
- at least one relevant reassurance or proof point.

Use:

- an outcome-focused H1;
- one short supporting paragraph;
- one dominant primary CTA;
- an optional quieter secondary CTA;
- a meaningful screenshot, result, example, or authentic image;
- a compact trust line.

Avoid abstract headlines that could describe hundreds of unrelated companies.

## Trust system

Use the strongest truthful evidence available:

1. Real product or result
2. Specific customer outcome
3. Third-party review
4. Named case study
5. Verifiable usage figure
6. Relevant certification
7. Transparent process or methodology
8. Genuine team or company information
9. Clear privacy and security explanation
10. Fair description of limitations

Match trust depth to commitment level:

- **Low commitment:** clarity, basic legitimacy, minimal privacy reassurance.
- **Medium commitment:** demo, exact pricing, reviews, compatibility, refund terms.
- **High commitment:** case studies, named team, references, process, scope, contracts.
- **Sensitive data:** data flow, storage, permissions, deletion, security model, legal limits.

Never invent evidence. Mark placeholders clearly.

## Copy rules

Prefer:

- concrete outcomes;
- specific verbs;
- short paragraphs;
- descriptive headings;
- active voice;
- measurable or observable claims;
- direct answers followed by explanation;
- language the customer already uses.

Replace vague copy:

- “Innovative solution” → state the actual result.
- “Fast and secure” → state what is fast and how data is handled.
- “Learn more” → label the destination or action.
- “Submit” → state what the submission accomplishes.

Every section should have one main idea.

## Visual and interaction rules

Use:

- clear hierarchy;
- restrained branding;
- consistent spacing;
- one primary action color;
- authentic visuals;
- readable typography;
- stable layouts;
- subtle functional motion;
- clear hover, focus, loading, success, and error states.

Avoid:

- hidden desktop navigation;
- excessive cards;
- multiple competing visual styles;
- generic stock imagery when real product visuals exist;
- scroll hijacking;
- autoplay audio;
- moving text;
- decorative animations that delay content;
- popups before the user receives value.

Interactions should help users:

- check compatibility;
- calculate cost;
- compare options;
- preview output;
- choose a use case;
- filter relevant content;
- book a time;
- upload an input;
- inspect an example;
- complete the task.

## Mobile requirements

On a normal phone viewport, users should quickly see:

- what the business offers;
- the primary result;
- the main CTA;
- one trust signal;
- an understandable menu.

Check for:

- tap target size;
- readable type;
- phone-number linking;
- sticky elements covering content;
- table usability;
- screenshot legibility;
- horizontal overflow;
- form keyboard behavior;
- cookie or chat overlays.

## Performance and accessibility

Target good Core Web Vitals:

- LCP at or below 2.5 seconds;
- INP at or below 200 milliseconds;
- CLS at or below 0.1.

Also require:

- semantic HTML;
- one clear H1;
- logical heading order;
- keyboard navigation;
- visible focus states;
- sufficient contrast;
- labelled fields;
- meaningful alt text;
- accessible errors;
- reduced-motion support;
- appropriately sized images;
- reserved media dimensions;
- minimal unnecessary JavaScript.

## SEO and AI readability

Optimize for successful intent satisfaction, not keyword repetition.

Use:

- direct answers near the top;
- descriptive titles and headings;
- clear entities and terminology;
- concise definitions;
- structured comparisons;
- step-by-step explanations;
- FAQs based on real objections;
- strong internal linking;
- original evidence and examples;
- transparent authorship and business information where relevant.

Do not split information across pages merely to generate clicks.

## Analytics and validation

Do not optimize only for bounce rate, time on page, or scroll depth.

Define successful actions such as:

- CTA click;
- call;
- form completion;
- booking;
- checkout;
- purchase;
- installation;
- download;
- activation;
- qualified lead;
- successful task completion.

Track meaningful events, not every possible interaction.

When reviewing behavior, remember:

- a fast exit may mean the user found the answer;
- long time on page may mean confusion;
- deep scrolling may mean the visitor could not find essential information.

Recommend:

- five-second comprehension tests;
- findability tests;
- task-completion tests;
- objection interviews;
- mobile testing;
- analytics segmented by page, source, and device;
- changing one important variable at a time.

## Audit workflow

When auditing an existing page:

1. Identify visitor intent and conversion.
2. Summarize the current information architecture.
3. Check first-viewport comprehension.
4. Check familiarity and navigation.
5. Check proof and risk reduction.
6. Check content order and progressive disclosure.
7. Check CTA clarity and friction.
8. Check mobile layout.
9. Check accessibility and performance risks.
10. Check SEO and AI-readable structure.
11. Identify missing information and unsupported claims.
12. Prioritize improvements by likely impact and effort.

Use these priority labels:

- **P0 — Blocking:** prevents understanding, trust, access, or conversion.
- **P1 — High impact:** likely to materially improve decisions or conversion.
- **P2 — Useful:** improves quality, clarity, or polish.
- **P3 — Optional:** experimentation or minor refinement.

## Build workflow

When creating a page or site:

1. State assumptions.
2. Define audience, intent, conversion, objections, and proof.
3. Produce the information architecture.
4. Produce a section-by-section wireframe.
5. Draft real copy, not lorem ipsum.
6. Define visuals and interactions.
7. Implement semantic, responsive components.
8. Add trust, accessibility, loading, empty, error, and success states.
9. Review against this skill.
10. Report remaining placeholders and evidence the owner must supply.

## Required output quality

When asked for a website plan, return:

- strategy;
- page goal;
- section order;
- section purpose;
- suggested copy;
- visual direction;
- CTA strategy;
- trust requirements;
- responsive considerations;
- measurement plan.

When asked to build, implement the strongest reasonable version directly.

When asked to audit, provide:

- concise diagnosis;
- prioritized findings;
- recommended new structure;
- replacement copy where needed;
- concrete implementation actions;
- final scorecard.

## Scorecard

Score from 1–10:

- Intent match
- First-viewport clarity
- Trust
- Information hierarchy
- Copy clarity
- Visual hierarchy
- CTA and conversion path
- Mobile usability
- Accessibility
- Performance
- SEO structure
- AI readability

Do not chase a numerical score mechanically. Explain the most important reasons behind each score and prioritize changes that improve real user success.
